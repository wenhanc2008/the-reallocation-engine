1 What This Mode Does and When to Use It

This mode is designed for a PhD student or postdoctoral researcher in economics, quantitative finance, or a related empirical social science who is transitioning to industry and is currently on OPT or preparing to file for H-1B sponsorship.

Core Problem — SOC Code Ambiguity: PhD economists are hired under at least five different SOC codes depending on how the employer frames the role. Each code has different H-1B prevailing wage floors, different LCA filing histories, and different cognitive-demand scores. Without knowing which code an employer uses, a candidate cannot correctly filter sponsorship history, set a wage floor, or assess AI-substitution risk.

Use this mode when:

  • You are 6–18 months from OPT expiration and need to prioritize by sponsorship timeline compatibility
  • You are targeting roles titled Economist, Research Scientist, Data Scientist, Quantitative Analyst, or Policy Analyst — different SOC codes with different sponsorship profiles
  • You want to separate genuinely research-oriented industry roles from analytics roles with higher AI-substitution risk
  • You need to verify whether a target employer has sponsored the specific SOC code relevant to your work, not just any tech or research role

Do not use this mode to:

  • Replace your own judgment about fit or role quality
  • Verify salary negotiation strategy (mode gives a floor, not a range)
  • Check visa eligibility for cap-exempt employers — those require manual verification

3 Existing Data Sources and Scripts

80 Days Layer

H-1B Sponsorship History

python scripts/filter_sponsors.py \
  --soc 19-3011 15-2041 15-2051 13-2099 19-3099 \
  --min_lca_count 3 \
  --years 3

Source: data/h1b_sponsors.csv. Filters employers with LCA filings in the relevant SOC codes over the past three years. Output: ranked list of employers with LCA count per SOC code.

Form D Funding Signal

python scripts/filter_form_d.py \
  --days 540 \
  --min_amount 5000000 \
  --output form_d_filtered.csv

Source: data/form_d.csv. Identifies employers with recent SEC Form D filings above $5M. Relevant for fintech, economic consulting boutiques, and policy startups. Not relevant for large public companies or federal employers.

Job-Ops Layer

ATS Provider Detection

node scripts/detect_ats.js --url [employer_careers_url]

Detects whether employer uses Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby, or a proprietary system. Relevant because many economist roles at consulting firms and think tanks are posted only on niche boards (ASSA, NBER, EconJobMarket) and never appear on aggregators.

Job Liveness Check

python scripts/check_liveness.py --urls job_urls.txt

Checks whether postings are still live before investing application effort. Economist roles at tech companies are often posted for 2–4 weeks and removed quickly.

Cognitive Pivot Layer

Role Resilience Score

python scripts/score_roles.py \
  --soc 19-3011 15-2041 15-2051 \
  --output cognitive_scores.csv

Source: data/soc_cognitive_scores.csv. Returns cognitive-demand and AI-substitution risk scores. Critical because 15-2051 scores differently from 19-3011 — a role coded as Data Scientist may carry measurably higher AI-substitution risk even if described in economic language.

4 Proposed New Data Sources (Not Yet in Repo)

1. Federal Reserve and Multilateral Institution LCA Filter

Status: Does not exist yet. Requires building data/federal_multilateral_employers.csv and filtering DOL public LCA disclosure files.

Rationale: Federal Reserve Banks, IMF, World Bank, BLS, CBO, and congressional research offices are disproportionately important for PhD economists but underrepresented in standard h1b_sponsors.csv because they file LCAs differently or are cap-exempt. This is the most important blind spot in the current repo for this population.

Source: DOL LCA disclosure data at dol.gov/agencies/eta/foreign-labor/performance, filtered by a curated list of ~40 federal and multilateral employers.

python scripts/filter_federal_econ_sponsors.py \
  --soc 19-3011 \
  --employer_list data/federal_multilateral_employers.csv \
  --output federal_econ_sponsors.csv

2. Tech Company Economist Team Tracker

Status: Does not exist yet. Requires curating data/tech_econ_employers.csv with ~25 companies.

Rationale: Amazon, Microsoft, Uber, Airbnb, Meta, Google, and Instacart all have established economist teams that sponsor under 19-3011. Their LCA history under 19-3011 needs to be separated from their much larger filings under 15-2051. A dedicated tracker would significantly improve signal for this candidate population.

python scripts/tech_econ_teams.py \
  --soc 19-3011 \
  --employer_list data/tech_econ_employers.csv \
  --output tech_econ_tracker.csv

3. BLS OEWS Metro-Level Wage Floor

Status: Does not exist yet. Requires downloading BLS OEWS metro-area tables and building a lookup by SOC code and MSA code.

Rationale: H-1B prevailing wage is metro-specific. A PhD economist in New York faces a different effective salary floor than one in Chicago or Boston under Level II and Level III LCA designations. The current repo uses national medians as a proxy.

Verified reference (BLS OES May 2024, national):

  • SOC 19-3011 Economists: median $115,440; mean $138,130
  • SOC 19-3011, Federal government: median $141,590
  • SOC 19-3011, Scientific R&D: median $129,430
python scripts/wage_floor.py \
  --soc 19-3011 \
  --metro "New York-Newark-Jersey City" \
  --level 2 \
  --output wage_floor.csv

6 Output Format

8 Domain Justification

Who Uses This Mode

A PhD student or recent graduate in economics, econometrics, or quantitative finance who is on OPT and targeting industry roles. My own situation is directly relevant: as an economist on OPT with a background in fiscal policy, financial conditions, and topological data analysis, I face exactly this problem. Roles I am qualified for span 19-3011 (economist teams at the Fed or consulting firms), 15-2051 (data science roles requiring empirical research capacity), and 19-3099 (policy research at think tanks). Without knowing which SOC code an employer has historically used, I cannot correctly filter sponsorship history.

Information Asymmetry Addressed

The central gap is that PhD economists cannot see how a given employer will classify their role in an LCA filing — and that classification determines the prevailing wage floor, the relevant LCA history to check, and the cognitive-demand profile of the work. A candidate might spend weeks pursuing an employer with extensive LCA history under 15-2051 but no history under 19-3011, which is a meaningfully different and potentially disqualifying outcome.

Engine Layer Connections

  • 80 Days to Stay: Sponsorship history filtered by the correct SOC codes, not just “tech company” or “has sponsored someone before”
  • Job-Ops: Liveness checking matters because economist roles at tech companies and consulting firms are often posted for 2–4 weeks and removed quickly; dead postings are a significant time sink
  • Cognitive Pivot: The SOC code determines the cognitive-demand score and AI-substitution risk; a role coded as 15-2051 has a measurably different risk profile than the same work coded as 19-3011

Failure Modes

Failure Mode 1 — SOC Code Misclassification (hardest to catch): If an employer files LCAs under 15-2051 for roles that are substantively identical to 19-3011 work, the mode shows zero LCA count for 19-3011 and incorrectly flags that employer as low probability. This error is invisible — the employer simply does not appear in filtered output rather than appearing with a wrong flag. A candidate who does not know to check both codes misses viable employers.
Failure Mode 2 — Federal and Multilateral Institutions (structural gap): Federal Reserve Banks, IMF, World Bank, CBO, and BLS are disproportionately important for PhD economists but show zero LCA count in standard data. For a PhD fiscal economist, this is the worst possible error because these are often the highest-quality sponsorship matches.
Failure Mode 3 — Stale LCA History: A company that sponsored 20 economists five years ago but froze headcount after 2022 tech layoffs still shows a HIGH LCA count. The mode cannot distinguish between an active economist team and one that has been dissolved.

What the Mode Missed and What I Would Change

The most significant miss is the NY Fed. The mode correctly flags it as a data gap but cannot resolve it. For a PhD fiscal economist, the NY Fed is one of the most relevant and realistic sponsorship matches — and the mode's inability to verify it is the primary limitation for this domain. Building filter_federal_econ_sponsors.py would be the highest-value addition for this candidate population.

Key Difference This Example Reveals

Form D is entirely irrelevant here. All four employers are nonprofits or federal agencies. They do not raise venture capital and will never appear in form_d.csv. The mode correctly skips the Form D check, but it means the startup-triage logic that is central to the engine's value proposition adds no signal for think tank and policy research candidates.

The DC think tank market for economists is essentially a three-source check: LCA history, posting liveness, and cognitive score. For this subpopulation, the mode is simpler but the federal employer gap is even more severe — the CBO, GAO, and congressional research offices are major employers and none appear reliably in standard LCA data.

What I Would Change

Add an explicit employer_type flag to the output table (Tech / Consulting / Think Tank / Federal / Startup) because application strategy, salary expectations, and relevant checks differ substantially across types even when LCA history looks comparable. A candidate who sees HIGH priority for both Amazon and Brookings needs to understand those require very different preparation, timeline expectations, and negotiation approaches.
