# Domain Justification: Data/AI OPT Sponsorship Triage

## 1. Who uses this mode and in what situation?
This mode is designed for international Master's or Ph.D. students in Data Science, Machine Learning, and Artificial Intelligence who are searching for jobs under the strict time pressures of OPT (90-day unemployment limit) or STEM OPT extensions. These students must secure roles that not only offer H-1B sponsorship but also strictly align with their STEM degree curriculum to pass USCIS scrutiny.

## 2. What information asymmetry does it address?
The core information asymmetry is the "AI-washing" of job titles in the tech industry. 

Currently, companies append terms like "AI," "ML," or "Data" to sales, marketing, and client-facing roles (e.g., "Strategic AI Account Executive" or "Product Marketing Manager, AI") to attract talent. An international student looking at a generic ATS portal cannot easily see the hidden danger: while a company might sponsor H-1B visas for its core engineers, it rarely sponsors visas for these "AI-adjacent" business roles because they often fail to meet the USCIS definition of a "specialty occupation" requiring a highly quantitative degree. Without this mode, a student might waste weeks applying to and interviewing for an "AI Solutions" role, only to find out at the offer stage that the company’s legal team will not sponsor it.

## 3. Connection to the Engine Layers
This mode actively connects all three layers of The Reallocation Engine to protect the student:
*   **Job-Ops Layer:** It ingests the raw, unverified pipeline of currently active job URLs from the ATS scraper.
*   **80 Days to Stay Layer:** It cross-references the hiring company against historical SEC/DOL H-1B data to prove whether the company actually has a history of approvals, and more importantly, whether their `top_job_titles_sponsored` includes technical data roles rather than just business roles.
*   **Cognitive Pivot Layer:** It connects target titles to BLS/SOC codes (e.g., SOC 15-2051 for Data Scientists) to establish a baseline for whether the role's cognitive demand aligns with a sponsor-eligible engineering track.

## 4. Specific Failure Modes (What it gets wrong)

**Failure Mode A: The "Solutions/Client-Facing" Trap (False Positives)**
*   *The Error:* The mode might flag a "Specialist Solutions Architect - ML" as `Manual Review` rather than `Skip` because it contains strong technical keywords. 
*   *Who it hurts most:* Desperate, entry-level students who lack industry experience. They might convince themselves the role is "technical enough" and accept an offer, only to have their STEM OPT extension denied by their university DSO or USCIS a year later because the actual daily duties were 80% client presentations and 20% coding.

**Failure Mode B: The Series A Startup Penalty (False Negatives)**
*   *The Error:* The mode might mark a highly relevant, deeply technical "Founding AI Engineer" role at a newly funded startup as having `No H-1B evidence found`.
*   *Who it hurts most:* High-achieving students targeting cutting-edge tech hubs (like SF or NYC). Because the startup is brand new, they haven't existed long enough to build an H-1B history in the 80-Days dataset. The mode assumes high risk based on missing data, potentially causing the student to miss out on an employer who is perfectly willing to sponsor but simply hasn't done it *yet*.
